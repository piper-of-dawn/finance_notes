Capital Asset Pricing Model

Some Definitions

  • Portfolio: Set of individual assets with their individual risks and return. We will assume that assets follow a normal distribution individually, so that we can boil down the analysis to the parameters that is mean and the variance.

  • Return at time \(t\), \(R_t\): It is given as \(R_t = {(P_t - P_{t-1}) + D_t} \over {P_{t-1}}\).

    • \(P_t\) denotes the price of the asset at time \(t\).
    • \(D_t\) denotes the dividend paid at the time \(t\)

  • Return at time \(t\), \(R_t\): It is given as \(R_i = {P_{ij} \over R_{ij}}\).
  • \(P_ij\) denotes the price of the asset \(t\) of the \(j_{th}\) portfolio.
  • \(D_t\) denotes the dividend paid at the time \(t\)

  • Variance of the asset \(i\) in portfolio \(j\): It is given as \(\sigma^2_{ij} = (\sum P_{ij} (R_{ij} - R_i)^2\).

Analysis

\(R_{p} = X_A\mu_A + X_B\mu_B\) and \(X_A + X_B = 1\).

\(\sigma_p^2 = [X_A^2\sigma_A^2 + X_B^2\sigma_B^2 + 2X_A(1-X_A)\rho_{AB}\sigma_A\sigma_B\). Here \(\rho_{AB}\) represents the correlation in returns in assets A and B.

If \(\rho_{AB} = 1\) then we have \(R_p = a + \sigma_p.b\) i.e. we have a linear relationship between the risk and the return.

Derivation: When \(\rho_{AB}=1\)

\(\sigma_P^2 = \sigma_A^2 {X_A}^2 + \sigma_B^2 {X_B}^2 + 2 \rho_{AB}. \sigma_A. \sigma_B. X_A. X_B\)

Substituting \(\rho_{AB} = 1\)we get,

\(\sigma_P = \sigma_A {X_A} + \sigma_B {X_B}\)

Since we only have two assets in the portfolio hence, \(X_A + X_B = 1\)

\(\Rightarrow \sigma_P = \sigma_A {X_A} + \sigma_B (1 - X_A)\)

Solving for \(X_A = {{\sigma_P - \sigma_B} \over {\sigma_A - \sigma_B}} \)

Hence the risk of the portfolio is a linear combination of the risk of the assets.

We can further write the return on the portfolio as: \(R_P = R_A.X_A + R_B.X_B \Rightarrow R_P = R_A.X_A + R_B.(1-X_A)\)

Substituting the value of \(X_A\), this gives \( R_P = R_A.{{\sigma_P - \sigma_B} \over {\sigma_A - \sigma_B}} + R_B.(1-{{\sigma_P - \sigma_B} \over {\sigma_A - \sigma_B}}) \)

Rearranging terms gives a linear relationship.

Capital Market Line

Solution to the portfolio problem

Consider a situation where investors can borrow or lend unlimited amount of money at a risk-free rate.

Let us consider a portfolio of riskless and risky asset.

The expected returns for such an portfolio would be given by: $$R_p = X R_a + (1-X)R_f$$.
Here \(R_f\) denotes the expected return on the risk free asset and \(X\) denotes the proportion invested in the risky asset..

Note that the risk-free asset has a zero variance. Hence the variance of the portfolio is given by: $$\sigma_p^2 = \sigma_a^2 X^2$$ $$\Rightarrow X = {\sigma_p \over \sigma_a} $$

The implication of \({\sigma_p \over \sigma_a}\) is that the ratio of risks of the portfolio is the proportion of money invested in risky asset.

When we substitute, \(X ={\sigma_p \over \sigma_a}\) in the expected returns to the portfolio equation \(R_p = X R_a + (1-X)R_f\), we get: $$R_p = {\sigma_p \over \sigma_a} (R_a - R_f) + R_f$$ Now on the risk-return plane this is the equation of the straight line with the intercept at \(R_f\) and slope \({(R_a - R_f) \over \sigma_p }\)

This straight line gives the efficient frontier. It shows all possible combinations of riskless and risk-free asset.

When we super-impose the risk preferences of the investor shown by the indifference curve we get the the solution to the portfolio problem represented by the point B.

This theorem has a special name, it is called Tobin Separation Theorem
