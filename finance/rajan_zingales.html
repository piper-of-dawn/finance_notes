What we know about Capital Structure

Introduction

Rajan and Zingales remark that a very little is known about the empirical relevance of theories. This is to say that there exist no empirical template grounded in first principles that could explain the theory. For example: In a MM world, leverage is not systematically related to any firm characteristic. Leverage is an outcome of accident, history or co-incidence in the Modigliani Miller world.

Objective of Rajan and Zingales Paper

Uses data on G7 countries on non-financial firms and non-service sector firms.

What are non financial firms?

Any firm that is not into serious business of lending or borrowing, that is they are not financial intermediaries. It is very difficult to seperate financial from non-financial firms.

Why we are not including financial firms?

Financial firms are by-definition always levered.Think of a bank that accepts deposits and gives out loans. Deposits are a kind of borrowing of a Bank from its customers. Hence a financial firm is always levered.

Why we are not including service sector firms?

Service sector firms usually do not require of very large fixed investment to begin unlike manufacturing firms which are dependent upon borrowing to fund the production. Service sector firms are more of human capital centric and since we do not have a theory that explains human capital hence we have decided to exclude the service sector firms.

The major takeaway here is that we do not have a theory of everything. No unified theory can explain the capital structure of firms and hence we have separate our sample into service / manufacturing firms, financial / non-financial firms etc.

Measurement of Leverage

One of the most remarkable explanations given by Rajan and Zingales is that they have very eloquently argued that which accounting measure of leverage one should use in the study is not a trivial question.

Leverage is nothing but stock of debt divided by the market value of firms. The broadest measure of leverage is total liabilities divided total assets.

Agency cost approach

This is manager-owner approach to view a from that is managers are different from owners. Hence it is suggested that the stock of debt should be divided by value of the firm to calculate leverage. This gives what is left over after for shareholders.

Bankruptcy cost approach

If the firm goes bankrupt then the ownership of the firm is transferred to the bondholders. This approach is that whether the firm then can meet its fixed payments along with bankruptcy cost. Hence the measure of interest coverage ratio becomes relevant

The takeaway here is the measurement metric is dependent on what you are trying to emphasize in the study. That is, if you want to emphasize bankruptcy costs then you should go with interest coverage ratio.

Problem with Total Liabilities / Total Assets

If we define leverage as total liabilities / total assets then we run into following practical problems:

  • There is no indication of whether the firm will default in future.
  • Total liabilities might include very short term liabilities (transaction purpose) such as accounts payable which might over state leverage. The accounts payable are mere postponement of payments not a medium to finance investment hence it should not be include for the calculation of leverage.Here, we are arguing that the duration of the debt is important.

    • Hence a more careful measure of leverage is required. Studies usually use: $$ \text{Leverage} = {\text{Debt(Both short term and long term)} \over \text{Total Assets}} $$

    This measure reflects the agency cost approach.

    $$ \text{Coverage Ratio} = {\text{EBIT} \over \text{Interest Expenses}} $$

    EBIT is the earning before interest and taxes. Coverage ratio measures the risk that equity holders will not be able to pay the fixed payments of the firm and hence would have to give up control. This measure reflects the bankruptcy cost approach.

    Problems that still remain

    However, in both the measures of leverage the problem of short term debts that just mean postponement of payments still remains.

    Ability of a firm to make fixed payments, at very low levels of debt is different from the firm which is at a very high level of debt.

    This measure is very sensitive to income fluctuations because both numerator and denominators have very sensitive to variables.

    Findings of the paper

    Tax story

    Before getting into anything we have to state some remarkable findings from Rajan and Zingales paper that US and Japan are very diverse countries in terms of their bank orientation but they have very similar leverages. Similarly United States and United Kingdom are very similar countries in terms of bank orientation they have very different leverages

    Hence we should depart from bank oriented explanations and focus on an institutions oriented study,

    The focus should be shifted onto tax codes, bankruptcy laws and bond market infrastructure to explain the capital structures.

    Tax stories are complicated. how operating profits are channeled to investors matters a lot in debt and equity decisions. It is quite possible that tax rates influence the aggregate leverage in the country. Rajan and Zingales find that this holds true for the high tax rates but not for the lower level of taxes.

    Bankruptcy story

    The contrast between United States and Germany is noteworthy here, laws in United States are very firm (debtor) friendly whereas in Germany they are very creditor friendly. So one can expect that a firm in United States that usually should not go bankrupt might go bankrupt.

    In United States, the priority is given to the continuity of firms where as in United Kingdom, the priority is given to the interests of the creditors.

    What doesn't matter?

    Bank Orientation and Market Orientation

    Germany is a very bank oriented economy that is the banks are allowed to lend as well as hold equities in the firms whereas in United States there is a restriction on both the activities.

    Rajan and Zingales conclude that the distinction between market oriented economies and Bank oriented economies is very clear in the type of financing that is used. Bank oriented economies go for more of a private financing (which cannot be traded) such as bank loans. such as which cannot be traded in the stock market, whereas Market oriented economies go for more of a public financing (bond markets) which can be publically traded.

    However note that this is not useful in explaining leverage. Debt is debt no matter if it is publicly traded or not publicly traded.

    Threat of takeover

    In United States United Kingdom and Canada the threat of takeover is very credible because of the diffused ownership structure. In Japan and Continental Europe the ownership structure is highly concentrated. Rajan and Zingales find that ownership or manager-owner dynamics do not impact leverage because both the opposite forces are at play here:

    1. When shareholders are in board of directors that is active part of management, then they will facilitate equity issues.
    2. On the other hand if shareholders are banks, then they will be against private financing and will force their clients to borrow.

    What matters

    Variables

    Rajan and Zingales distil down to these four factors to explain leverage:

    1. Firm Size: Leverage is expected to be positively correlated with firm size as large firms have an advantage of collateral and information symmetry over small firms wrt obtaining debt.
    2. Tangibility of assets i.e. Fixed assets / Total : More fixed assets you have more collateral you have for obtaining debt and thus reduces agency cost of debt (Burger and Udell (1994))
    3. Market to book ratio
    4. Profitability

    Model Specification

    $$ L_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 S_i + \beta_2 T_i + \beta_3 MB_i + \beta_4 P_i + \epsilon_i$$ Here,
  • Firm Size: \(S\)
  • Tangibility of assets: \(T\)
  • Market to book ratio: \(MB\)
  • Profitability: \(P\)
  • \(\epsilon_i\) denotes the \(i_{th}\) firm level shocks.

    • Measure of leverage used is Debt to Capitalisation ratio.

    The coefficients are estimated using the censored Tobit model.

    Regression results:

  • Size is positively correlated with leverage.
  • Tangibility is positively correlated with leverage.
  • Profitability is negatively correlated with leverage.
  • Market to book ratio is negatively correlated with leverage.

    • Criticism

    This empirical work lacks any theoretical underpinning.
