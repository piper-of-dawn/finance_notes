Financial Constraint

The finance constraint stems from the assymetric information between investors and entrepreneurs. Investors face a moral hazard that entrepreneurs have an incentive to misallocate. Financial Constraint here implies informational cost faced by entrepreneurs.

How finance constraint in Marris model is different?

Finance constraint in Marris Growth Model stems from the risk of firm being taken over. If managers of the firm get over-board with the firm growth at the cost of dividend payouts - the firm is exposed to the risk of being taken over.

Empirical Strategy

So we have observed that in Imperfect Capital Markets:

  • Uncollateralized external financing is more costly than internal financing.​
  • Holding constant investment opportunities, a reduction in net worth reduces investment for firms facing information costs.​

    • The empirical strategy is to access whether the neoclassical investment criterion holds for firms facing low information costs while failing for firms facing high information costs, and to measure effects of changes in net worth on investment for firms with high information costs.​ If MM assumptions are satisfied, real firm decisions (ie investment) motivated by the maximization of shareholders claims, are independent of financial factors such as liquidity leverage or dividend payments. This provided the underpinning of the neoclassical theory of investment-Q theory.

    Empirical Challenges

    Most empirical work does not assign any relevance to financial factors. For example: CAPM does not assign any importance to anything except \(beta\).

    But in reality, investment is sensitive to financial factors and these financial factors might not be constant across firms and might differ. Since firms are not homogenous, we might need to move beyond the story of a representative firm.

    We move beyond the assumption of representative form by examining:

  • Historical Data: We need to analyse the past episodes where reduction in net worth resulted in reduction in investment.
  • Categorisation of Firms: The firms can be categorised into, "high information cost" and "low information cost.

    • The following challenges need to be addressed:

    1. Measuring Investment opportunities: This we have already solved by measuring Tobin's Q
    2. Identifying Constrained and Unconstrained firms: Constrained firms are those who are facing an upward facing supply curve with the kink (at \(W_0\)) and unconstrained firms are those which face a horizontal supply curve.

      3. Why Constrained?

      Their capital stock is much lower than \(K^*\), they cannot reach their preferred capital stock because of informational problems.

    3. Identifying changes in Net Worth: We need a proxy to identify changes in net-worth. Most empirical studies use a firm's cash flow as a proxy for the change in net worth. The maintained assumption in such an approach is that the component of shifts in net worth accounted for by changes in expected future profitability should be captured in a measure of investment opportunities

    If all other financial factors are un-important (statistically insignificant) then internal and external financing are perfect substitutes of each other.

    Such an a priori grouping strategy does not yield an ideal test of the underlying models for at least two reasons.

  • First, the strategy relies on only imperfect and imprecise proxies for net worth and the magnitude of information costs.
  • Second, information costs of screening and monitoring vary over time for a given firm. That is, problems in ascertaining the quality or riskiness of a firm's projects presumably become less severe as a firm matures and its reputation develops (reducing the slope of the \(S\) curve.

    Defn

    If the cost of internal and external financing is substantially different from each other then we are in an imperfect information world. This implies that substantial amount of dividends in presence of good investment opportunities goes against value maximisation of the firm.

    This makes a perfect intuitive sense. In an imperfect information world - the internal finance is always the cheaper source of funds and hence if the investment opportunities are promising then the firm should always retain profits rather than paying out dividends, because adjusting dividends in very easy.

    This makes dividend policy irrelevant for the firms which are in a perfect information environment. Hence firms in which financing constraints are binding are highly sensitive to the cash-flows.

    Estimation of Net Worth

    Note that, net worth is proxied by the cash flow and given that error terms are well behaved then any correlation between the net-worth proxied by cash flow and investments violate the assumptions of a perfect information frictionless model.

    Re-iterating that in a perfect information world where there isn't any difference in internal and external funds, net worth itself is irrelevant.

    Model Specification

    FHP(1988) estimate the following equation: $${I \over K}_{it} = a_i + b \ Q_{it} + c \ {(CF \over K}_it +\epsilon_{it} $$
    • \({I \over K}_{it}\) is investment for firm \(i\) at time \(t\).
    • \(a_i\) is the firm-level fixed effect.
    • \({Q}_{it}\) is the Tobin's Q for firm \(i\) at time \(t\).
    • \({CF \over K}_{it}\) is the cashflow (net worth) for firm \(i\) at time \(t\).
    • \(\epsilon_{it}\) is white noise.

    The coefficient \(c\) should not be significantly different from zero to imply a frictionless world. If \(c\) is significant then capital market imperfections exist. \(a_i\) represents firm level fixed effects which are time-invariant.

    Empirical Methods and Results from FHP Paper

    To implement the firm classification by retention behaviour FHP group firms into three categories i.e. high, medium and low dividend payout firms.

    FHP found a significant \(c\) in all the three groups.

    FHP found significantly larger estimated cashflow coefficient for low dividend payout firms. That is controlling for Q (investment opportunities), \(I \over K\) is influenced more by cashflow (\(c\)) is higher for low dividend payout firms.

    This result makes an intuitive sense as low dividend payout firms retain profits because they are financially constrained.

    FHP concluded that financing constraints play a much important role in investment decisions of firms.
