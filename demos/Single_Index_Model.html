Single Index Model and Capital Asset Pricing Model

Definitions:

The return on a stock can be written as: $$ R_i = a_i + \beta_i.R_m $$ where:

  • \(a_i\) is the component of security i’s return that is independent of the market’s performance — a random variable.
  • \(R_m\)s the rate of return on the market index — a random variable.
  • \(\beta_i\) is a constant that measures the expected change in \(R_i\) given a change in \(R_m\).
  • \(\sigma_m \) denote the standard deviation of \(R_m\).
  • \(\sigma_{ei} \) denote the standard deviation of \(i_{th}\) security.

Interpretation of \(\beta\)

\(\beta\) represents the sensitivity of the returns to a stock to market movements. If \(\beta\) is greater than 1 then stock is more sensitive to the market and if the \(\beta\) is less than 1, the stock is less sensitive to the market.

Why 1?
Regress the portfolio to itself and you will get \(\beta\) = 1

Let us break \(a_i\) into the intercept \(\alpha_i\) and the random component \(e_i\). \(a_i = \alpha_i + e_i\). Now the return on a stock can be written as: $$ R_i = \alpha_i + \beta_i.R_m + e_i$$

Expected Return of an individual security
$$ E(R_i) = E(\alpha_i) + E(\beta_i.R_m) + E(e_i) $$
Risk of an individual security
$$ Var(R_i) = Var(\alpha_i) + Var(\beta_i.R_m) + Var(e_i) $$ $$ \Rightarrow \sigma_i^2 = \beta_i^2 \sigma_m^2 + \sigma_ei^2 $$

Risk of the Portfolio

Define \(\beta_p\) as the weighted average of \(\beta_i\)s, i.e. \(\beta_p = \sum \beta_i X_i \).

Define \(\alpha_p\) as the weighted average of \(\alpha_i\)s, i.e. \(\alpha_p = \sum \alpha_i X_i \).

$$ R_p^* = \alpha_p + \beta_p.R_m^* $$

Diversification

Risk of the portfolio \(\sigma_p\)^2 is: $$ \sigma_p^2 = \beta_p^2 \sigma_m^2 + \sum_{i}^{N} X_i^2 \sigma_{ei}^2 $$ Let \(X_i\) be \(1 \over N\), means we allocate equal amount of money to each portfolio. $$\Rightarrow \sigma_p^2 = \beta_p^2 \sigma_m^2 + {1 \over N} \sum_{i}^{N} {1 \over N} \sigma_{ei}^2 $$ Now as \(N \to \infty\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(\sigma_{ei}^2 \to 0\).

The implication being as the size of portfolio increases the average residual risk decreases. This is known as diversification. The risk that cannot be diversified is the first term: \(\beta_p^2 \sigma_m^2\).

Hence, \(\sigma_p = \sum \beta_i X_i \sigma_m\). Note that \(\sigma_m \) denotes the standard deviation of \(R_m\), and hence is a given parameter regardless of the security we examine.

Therefore, \(\sigma_ei\) is known as diversifiable risk and \(\sigma_m\) is known as non-diversifiable risk.

Price of Risk

We have seen that the capital market line is given by \(R_p = {{(R_a - R_f)} \over \sigma_a}. \sigma_p + R_f \).

Risk free rate \(R_f\) is the price of time.

Note that here \({R_a - R_f} \over {\sigma_a}\) represents the price of risk.The interpretation is that it represents the marginal returns that portfolio will pay when risk is increased.

If we are anywhere on the capital market line we are combining the risk-free asset and the portfolio in a particular combination.

Any portfolio above the capital market line will either have a higher return at a same/lower risk. Hence investors will short the portfolio lying on CML for that portfolio.

Thus arbitrage will ensure that all the investments and the portfolios lie on CML.

We know, $$ R_f = a $$ $$ R_a = R_f + b $$ $$ \Rightarrow R_i = R_f + \beta_i (R_a - R_f)$$
