Marris Growth Model

Growth of Firms

Introduction

  1. Marris Growth Model is a steady state growth model.
  2. Growth of firms assets represent supply.
  3. Growth of Demand is sales value growth
  4. Value of assets is measured by its replacement value.
  5. Succesful diversification of products is important for firm growth. Diversification is lynchpin of longterm growth.

Therefore in order to grow faster than existing market growth rate, firms must carry out successful diversification.

Growth of Demand

Growth of demand is given as: $$G_d = f_1(D)$$

  • \(G_d\) represents the growth of demand
  • \(D\) is a metric for Diversification

Growth of Supply

Growth of Supply \(G_s\) is the investment as a proportion of capital Therefore $$G_s = {\alpha \pi \over K}$$

  • Investment is a function of profit \( \pi\). It is some constant \( \alpha\) times the profits \( \pi\).
  • \(K\) represents the existing capital stock

Let profit per unit capital stock be represented by \(p\). Therefore, $$ p = {\pi \over K}$$

Diversification

Diversification is an inverse function of \(p\).

Hence we can write \(D\) as: $$ D = g({1 \over p})$$

This gives the growth of demand \(G_d\) as: $$ G_d = f(g({1 \over p}))$$

Let \(Q\) represent the output of the firm.

\(p\) further can be written as: $$ p = {\pi \over K} = {\pi \over Q}.{Q \over K}$$

Let \({\pi \over Q}\) = \(m\) and \({K \over Q}\) = \(v\).

Hence $$ G_d = f(g({v \over m}))$$

Diversification is inversely related to \(m\).

\({K \over Q}\) = \(v\). So \({1 \over v}\) represents the rate of return on capital. If \(v\) rises the rate of return on capital falls. Hence diversification is inversely related to rate of returns on capital.

Finance Constraint

We know that a higher \(\alpha\) increases the threat of takeover because, higher \(\alpha\) is associated with lower dividend. This acts as a self-imposed finance constraint to the managers.

We assume that shareholders are only bothered about the current and future dividends and capital gains.

Therefore, if we hold the retention ratio constant, the current dividends are determined by current profits and growth in dividend are determined by growth rate of profits.

The current price of a share equals: $$S_0 = \sum_{t=0}^{n-1} [{{K_0.p.(1-r). (1+g)^t} \over {N(1+ \delta)^{t+1}}}]+ {S_n \over (1 + \delta)^n}$$

    Let us break this equation down:
  • \(K_0\) represents capital invested in period 0.
  • \(p\) is the expected profit rate. It represents expected rate of return on capital = \({\pi \over K}\) or the profit rate.
  • \(r\) represents the retention ratio.
  • \(\delta\) is the discount rate.
  • \(g\) is the growth rate of the firm.
  • \(N\) is the number of shares.
  • \(n\) is the number of time periods.
  • \(S_n\) is when share is sold in period \(n\ ).

Note that the first term, \(\sum_{t=0}^{n-1} [{{K_0.p.(1-r). (1+g)^t} \over {N(1+ \delta)^{t+1}}}]\) represents the present value of the future stream of dividends per share, assuming that dividends are paid at the end of each period.
  • The numerator \(K_0.p.(1-r). (1+g)^t\) shows the profits multiplied by the growth (future profits). \((1-r)\)is the unretained profit or the dividend.
  • If the dividend \(K_0.p.(1-r) \over N \) is constant over time then share must grow at the same rate as the dividend. $$ S_n = S_0(1+g)^n$$
  • The denominator \(N(1+ \delta)^{t+1}\) is dividing by number of shares to calculate per share and the discount rate to calculate the present value.
The second term, \(S_n \over {(1 + \delta)^n}\) represents the present value of the share when it is sold at the beginning of the period \(t\) = \(n\).

The \(\delta\) \(>\) \(g\) must be true. Why? Because if the inequality is reversed we will have dividend which grows infinitely.

With some more algebraic manipulations, we have $$S_0 = {{K_0.p.(1-r)} \over {N (\delta - g)}}$$
